Introduction & Project Goals

As one of the few kosher restaurants in my hometown of Riverdale, New York, Kai Fan has long been a go-to option for delivery. I chose to redesign Kai Fan’s website to address its outdated design, confusing navigation, and poor visual hierarchy, all of which created significant barriers to an enjoyable user experience

My overarching goal was to build a modern, responsive, and visually appealing interface that prioritizes user needs. By streamlining navigation and ensuring clarity in design, I aimed to help customers seamlessly find essential info—like hours, location, and menu options—while reflecting Kai Fan’s brand identity.

Usability Analysis

Using five core criteria—Efficiency, Memorability, Learnability, Conceptual Model, and Accessibility—I analyzed how the original Kai Fan website impacts user interactions and where it falls short. Expand each section below to see the specific issues discovered.

  • The “A Destination” section tons of space with only two lines of text, wasting valuable screen real estate while also looking visually unnapealing
  • Multiple "Order Online" buttons create confusion about which one to use.
  • The site lacks a built-in menu page—users must click “Order Online,” leading to an external site and adding friction for simple menu browsing.
  • Key info like business hours and location is buried at the bottom, forcing users to scroll and search for basic details.
  • On mobile, unoptimized images slow loading times, further reducing efficiency.
  • Repetitive promotional text (“All you can eat sushi is back…”) clutters the homepage, making it harder to find important information.
  • Text-heavy promotions precede essential details (like hours, location, and food options), making users repeatedly hunt for key info.
  • Large image captions (“FRESH INGREDIENTS,” “MASTER CHEF”) appear interactive but aren't clickable, creating confusion on subsequent visits. They don't add anything important, yet make the site confusing.
  • A lack of a distinctive color scheme or typography makes it difficult for returning users to recognize Kai Fan’s site from others.
  • The “Order Online” button is not very noticeable, it blends into the background due to a thin white outline that contrasts poorly. Users may not recognize it as a call to action button.
  • No hover effects or animations guide a first-time user’s attention, missing a key opportunity for to aid site learning.
  • The prominent “All you can eat sushi” banner competes visually with key navigation elements but doesn’t clarify how to take advantage of the deal in-person or online.
  • Outdated visual hierarchy leads to multiple large text elements vying for attention, leaving newcomers unsure where to look first.
  • Users expecting a "Menu" page may not realize that the "Order Now" button is where the menu is found. This slows down a newcomer looking to learn the site structure.
  • No clear color-coding or guidance leaves new users to figure out the site on their own.
  • The site funnels menu browsing into an external ordering system, breaking users’ expectations of viewing a menu in-page. Menu and ordering should be front and center.
  • Large promotions precede basic context, forcing users to scroll past slogans and images to find essential restaurant details.
  • Static or misleading elements ( “CRAFTED IN THE KITCHEN”) look clickable but lead nowhere, confusing the mental model of how the site should work.
  • The hidden main menu deviates from standard restaurant sites, potentially causing users to miss or misunderstand the available pages.
  • Irrelevant paragraphs and unappetizing pictures further distort the intended mental model of a welcoming, informative restaurant page.
  • Contrast issues: White or light-colored text over background images fails WCAG guidelines, making text hard to read for low-vision users.
  • Redundant links and missing alternative text on images impair screen reader navigation and comprehension.
  • Although some ARIA titles are present, the overall site structure lacks consistent labeling for assistive technologies.

Visual Redesign Process

I structured my redesign around four key stages: establishing a style guide, creating detailed mockups for multiple breakpoints, coding the layout, and testing for usability and accessibility.

This iterative approach helped ensure that each design decision was validated through user feedback and testing. By the end, I had a cohesive redesign that addressed the major pain points identified during the initial analysis.

Style Guide

The redesign relies on a color palette, pairing teal with dark grey and white. Roboto, Open Sans and Raleway were primarily used for texts. Buttons, links, and hover states follow consistent design rules, making interactions predictable and coherent.

Mockups

I produced high-fidelity mockups for mobile, tablet, and desktop. Each design variation uses consistent elements—like the navigation bar and color scheme—but reflows them to fit the available screen space.

The mockup featured intuitive, responsive layouts with reusable components, consistent visual cues, clear transitions across screen sizes, and accommodations for diverse user actions.

How Problems Were Solved

I implemented improvements to address the key issues identified in our analysis:

  • Efficiency: Redundant elements were eliminated and the navigation streamlined so that essential details like operating hours and location are immediately visible. Unnecessary content was removed to reduce friction.
  • Learnability: Micro-interactions, hover effects, and clear visual cues were introduced. Updated labels and guidance help new users quickly understand how to interact with the site.
  • Memorability: A distinctive color palette and updated typography were adopted. A persistent, intuitive navigation bar ensures returning visitors can easily recognize and recall the brand.
  • Conceptual Model: Content was reorganized to match user expectations. Misleading or static elements were removed to create a logical, intuitive flow throughout the site.
  • Accessibility: Color contrast was improved, descriptive alt text was added, and interactive elements were optimized to meet WCAG guidelines, making the site more inclusive for users with disabilities.

These enhancements transformed the site into a user-friendly and effective digital experience.

Reflections

This project was a fun excersize that taught me several critical lessons:

  • Attention to Detail: Subtle choices like precise color contrasts and micro-interactions can make a huge difference.
  • User Focused Design: Aligning design decisions with real user needs and mental models is very important.
  • Balance of Aesthetics and Functionality: The project highlighted the necessity of creating a visually appealing interface without sacrificing usability or accessibility.

This was my first site redesign and I enjoyed looking at a site, assessing its strengths and weaknesses, and then fixing it myself!
